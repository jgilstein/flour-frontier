Field Insights

What the field added

The spreadsheets showed where the opportunities were. The conversations explained why some of them were realistic, why others were not, and what would actually need to change for GMI to win more of the business. This page pulls together the territory read from the FSI team and the AEs who work these markets every day.

Why it mattered

What the field changed

The market work narrowed the map. The field work sharpened the recommendation. Across the conversations, the same themes kept surfacing: freight matters more than flour price in many markets, some opportunities need a better product fit and not just a better price, and a few operating rules such as the three-truck minimum can shut GMI out of direct business that would otherwise make sense.

The conversations also changed the tone of the project. They made it less about abstract whitespace and more about where GMI can realistically win with the current network, the right distributor partner, and the right product line.

One pattern came up repeatedly. In some bakery segments, the issue is not only freight or price. Product fit matters. Across multiple territories, ethnic bread and bakery applications raised questions about dough stability, cleaner-label needs, shelf life, and whether enzyme-based systems perform better than malt-based ones. That does not change the market ranking, but it does change which opportunities are realistic.
Cross-market themes

What kept repeating

Freight decides a lot. This was clearest in the West and Southeast. In many cases GMI was not losing because of flour quality. It was losing because local mills or closer facilities could land product cheaper.
Product fit matters more than the portfolio sometimes allows. Cleaner-label, untreated, unmalted, enzyme-based, shelf-life-oriented, and application-specific flours came up across regions.
The ethnic bakery opportunity is broader than one market. Hispanic, Middle Eastern, Cuban, Lebanese, Russian, and other bakery communities all came up in different forms depending on territory.
The direct minimum is too high. The three-truck minimum came up in multiple territories as a barrier. A two-truck minimum would reopen a set of profitable, low-maintenance direct accounts.
Some opportunities are not worth chasing. The field was clear that niche one-offs and ultra-artisan self-milling accounts can soak up time without scaling.
Leadership and support

What the team around the work added

Cathy Meyer · Director, NAF Ingredients
Cathy kept the project grounded in what the business can realistically support. Her view was that the answer had to work inside the current network, hold up against what the field sees every day, and lead to actions the team could actually take. That kept the work focused on where GMI should lean in, not just where the map looked interesting.
James Mueting · Zone Manager, FSI West
James was a strong point of contact throughout the project and gave me a clear place to bring back what I was hearing from the AEs. That made it easier to pressure-test field insights, connect them back to the broader question, and turn a long list of conversations into a recommendation the team could actually use.
Melissa Tiemeyer · East Zone Manager
Melissa’s perspective helped tie the territories together. Orlando already proved what a better-positioned warehouse can do, and Atlanta is the next logical step. She also reinforced that the team is talented but spread thin, which makes time allocation and support structure part of the answer, not just map scoring.
Catherine Colon · Senior Sales Operations Manager
Catherine brought the sales-operations view to the project. Her perspective was portfolio-wide: how pricing works, where freight changes the economics, and which markets are worth sustained support. She reinforced that flour and mixes behave differently. Flour can reprice with the market every two weeks, while mix pricing carries freight in a much more fixed delivered structure. She also pointed to Florida as proof that a focused market bet can work when GMI stays committed behind it.
Territory reads

What the AEs said by market

Nancy Muller · Southern California
Nancy’s view was that Southern California is not just a freight problem. It is also a portfolio-fit problem. In the West, cleaner-label, untreated, unmalted, and enzyme-treated flours matter more, especially in artisan, Middle Eastern, and other specialty bakery applications. Better targets were practical ones: universities like UCLA, larger ethnic bakery pockets, and direct accounts that could work at a two-truck rhythm instead of today’s three-truck minimum.
Shotsie Wilson · Mountain West & Southwest
Across a nine-state territory, Shotsie runs on distributor relationships and accessibility, leaning on Shamrock as the bellwether. Las Vegas, Salt Lake City, and Wyoming are more underserved than the map alone suggests, and growth may keep shifting north of an increasingly expensive Colorado. Her strongest point was product direction: enzyme-based, cleaner-label, tortilla-oriented, and smaller-bag formats are where the market is moving.
Yoel Sharabi · West Coast
Freight, not flour price, decides much of the West. In the Bay Area, local mills are too close and too cheap to ignore, which makes the economics difficult for GMI even when the product is right. The better opening is in Oregon, Washington, and the mid-sized cities between Sacramento and Portland, where operators value samples, troubleshooting, and a steady relationship more than the absolute lowest book price.
Gus Patrick · South Texas
Houston is a real mixed-market opportunity with scale, but pricing and mill proximity are the constant barrier. Large bakery accounts can come down to a BakeMark or Ardent price GMI cannot meet even at very low margin. The upside is still there, especially if the low-to-mid protein story improves and the tortilla position gets sharper. The market wants cleaner-label solutions, but it also needs realistic pricing.
Melissa Magsalin · Texas & Oklahoma
Independent distributors carry this market. Lasanti Foodservice out of Dallas keeps growing and pulls Gold Medal and All Trumps deep into pizzerias and Italian kitchens. Where GMI loses, it often loses on price against nearby millers rather than on quality. The Sysco North Texas loss alone moved a meaningful amount of flour, but Dallas is still growing and the market remains worth rebuilding around.
Josh Cohen · Southeast
Pizza, bakery, and manufacturing demand is all there in the Southeast, but Ardent in Chattanooga and local alternatives set the price to beat. Atlanta remains the network unlock. He also pointed to product-fit problems in soft wheat and ethnic bakery uses, where the right formula matters as much as the route to market.
Jeffrey Santiago · Florida
Florida is one of the clearest places where product fit and closer support both matter. Distributors have stayed loyal, and operators will work with GMI when the product performs. But some bread applications, especially in Hispanic bakery uses, raise real formula questions around dough stability, fermentation, and shelf life. Orlando proved what closer support can do, so well that it has started to run into its own capacity.
Chuck Bootier · Greater New York
The bromate transition is the defining issue in his market. His practical view was to replace bromate with ascorbic acid while changing as little as possible around the bag and presentation, since customers react to visible changes first. He also reinforced that ethnic bakery demand is real and durable, but it depends on having the right flour for the application. Mixes remain a difficult price fight against Dawn.
Dean Psyhogios · Midwest (IL-IN-OH-KY-MI)
Dean’s territory reinforced three themes: Hispanic and Cuban bakery demand is real, relationship and spec accuracy can still beat pure price in some accounts, and GMI is structurally stronger in bag than in bulk. He also pointed to the same operating lever others did: a two-truck minimum would make more direct business realistic.
Kevin Karbo · Western PA & Western NY
Kevin’s read on Pittsburgh was practical: start with the distributors already moving meaningful volume and focus first on the accounts that matter most. He also pointed to a product gap outside straight flour, especially in shelf-life bakery items where enzyme-based solutions could help GMI compete more effectively in donuts, buns, and other retail bakery products.
What it changed

How the conversations changed the recommendation

The field made the Texas recommendation more realistic, the Southeast recommendation more credible, the Houston case more specific, and the West recommendation much more nuanced. It also added something the map alone could not: a practical read on which opportunities are worth pursuing, which ones need a better product fit first, and which operating rules are getting in GMI’s way.

That is why the final recommendation does not read like a market-sizing exercise. It reads like a set of decisions the business can actually make.
