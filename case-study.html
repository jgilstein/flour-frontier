Case Study

Where flour focus belongs

The full analysis behind the recommendation: the question, the data, the scoring, the stress tests, and a plan built to run inside the network we already have.

The Question

What was actually asked

Look ten years out and say where flour growth should come from, using the current network and no new mill. That last part was the whole strategy. With a new mill off the table, the real choices became the ones we can actually make now: which distributors to deepen, where a warehouse would help, and which selling playbooks to copy from one market into the next.

The Approach

Five steps from data to answer

I sized the fifty largest metros against our mills and warehouses, read our own account-level volume and value, scored each market on six factors, and then put the ranking in front of the AEs who own these territories. Their context turned numbers into reasons and changed the framing in real ways. The final recommendation came out of both.

The Network Reality

Why an Atlanta warehouse keeps coming up

Most of this plan runs inside what we already operate: five mills and six warehouses. The Southeast is the exception. Atlanta, Tampa, Charlotte, and Nashville sit in the strongest region in the data with no warehouse close enough to serve them well, and every Southeast AE pointed to the same fix. When product sits closer, the freight gap that keeps us from competing mostly disappears. Orlando already proved it, so well that it is now bumping against its own capacity.

An honest note on cost. A new mill would run about $100 million and is off the table, confirmed early with Cathy Meyer. An Atlanta warehouse is a different and much smaller question, but it is not free. It should be studied on its own cost-to-serve case rather than waved through. It is simply the one network investment the evidence keeps pointing toward.
The Recommendation

Four moves, built for the network we have

Rebuild Texas around the market
Houston & Dallas
The Sysco distributor-label business went to Ardent last fall and is not realistically winnable in the near term. But Dallas is still growing about 5.3% a year and our branded flour is holding across Texas distributors. The work is to protect that momentum, grow the independent and Hispanic bakery base, and stay close enough to step back in when Sysco wants a second source again.
Owners: Gus Patrick, Melissa Magsalin
Win the Southeast
Atlanta, Tampa, Charlotte, Nashville
This is the strongest region in the data and the one every Southeast AE points to. The biggest single unlock is an Atlanta warehouse, which would ease freight across all four markets at once and make the Gordon Food Service relationship easier to grow, building on the momentum already running through Shepherdsville.
Owners: Josh Cohen, Dean Psyhogios, Jeffrey Santiago
Build the Houston case
Houston
Houston is a 60.3 million pound market where we hold less than half our national share. Closing even part of that gap is worth roughly 9 million pounds a year from a single city. Scope a focused plan with Gus Patrick aimed at the independent operator base.
Owner: Gus Patrick
Grow the West where it pays
Phoenix, Pacific Northwest, SoCal
The West is losing share on freight, but the clearest growth looks more relationship-led than broad-based, especially in Oregon, Washington, and the mid-sized cities between Sacramento and Portland. Hold Phoenix as the anchor, and recognize that some West Coast opportunities will also require a more intentional specialty and clean-label flour position for independent ethnic food marts, bakeries, and other specialty operators.
Owners: Yoel Sharabi, Nancy Muller, Shotsie Wilson
What Comes Next

The first thing to pick up

Start with a real cost-to-serve case for the Atlanta warehouse. It is the move with the widest reach and the one the field keeps returning to. From there: rebuild Texas around the market, a Houston growth plan with Gus Patrick, and a West Coast playbook built on the relationship-led approach Yoel and Nancy described.

One follow-up worth separating from the market work is product fit. Several AE conversations pointed to cases where the issue is not only freight or price, but whether the flour or bakery formula matches the application well enough to win. That came up in Hispanic bread applications, Southeast soft wheat applications, and retail bakery shelf-life needs. That question is especially important in the West, where cleaner-label, untreated, unmalted, and enzyme-based flours are in, yet they do not always fit the current portfolio. Some of the next opportunity may require product adjustment, not just better coverage.
