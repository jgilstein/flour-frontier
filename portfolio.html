Section 2

The brief

James Mueting, my Zone Manager FSI West, framed the project on day one. Look ten years out. Figure out where NAF FSI should focus flour milling and warehouse capacity. Back the recommendation with data. Operate within the current network. No new mill capex.

The General Mills fiscal year starts June 1, which means the recommendations land in the middle of FY27 Q1. The 90 day actions in Phase 1 execute through Q1 and into early Q2.

The headline reality the project needed to absorb. Flour is 19M+ EQC of annual distribution with roughly flat year over year volume. The number hides a mixed story. Branded Sack and Distributor Label are 75% of the channel. Distributor Label is slipping across every major distributor. The strategic question is not "where is the market" but "where do we put our energy first, with what we already have."

Section 3

The approach

Six analytical phases over ten weeks.

Phase 1: Pulled the top 50 US metros from Circana, NIQ, and Nielsen DMA rankings. Built a baseline of population, projected growth, foodservice density, and current GM presence.

Phase 2: Joined that list to GM's mill and warehouse footprint with pairwise distances. Identified the supply chain gaps.

Phase 3: Layered GMI Region Full and Account Full data from Tableau, broken down by AE, distributor, and metro. Year over year EQC change at the account level.

Phase 4: Added Circana TTM data at the US Census Region level (Northeast, Central, South, West) to validate the metro signal against the regional one.

Phase 5: Built a six dimension scoring framework. Market Opportunity, Market Health, Future Growth, HQ Inflow, Trajectory Proof, Transport Quality. Normalized 1 to 5 within the True 5 group. Composite produces the final ranking.

Phase 6: Pressure tested the ranking against four sensitivity lenses and against the Account Executives who actually own the territories.

Section 4

What the data said

Four findings shaped the recommendation.

Finding 1
Houston is the biggest accessible opportunity in the country
Houston is a 60.3M lb DMA where GM holds GMI Index 49, less than half national parity. The gap is roughly 8 to 9 million pounds of annual flour we are leaving on the table. No other True 5 has a TAM gap that big.
Finding 2
Texas is execution, not market
Sysco Central TX is down 38% YoY and Sysco North TX is down 36% YoY. But the underlying DFW market is growing +5.3% YoY. This is recoverable customer execution, not a market in decline. Top 5 customer losses in the entire NAF book.
Finding 3
Atlanta GFS is already winning, double down
While the overall Atlanta DMA flour market declined, GMI through GFS Shepherdsville grew +122.5% YoY. The recommendation in Phase 2 is not theoretical, it is the live trajectory. Dean Psyhogios owns this and is already executing.
Finding 4
An Atlanta warehouse would change the whole Southeast equation
The single biggest network move available is the proposed Atlanta warehouse. It would compress cost-to-serve for the entire Southeast corridor (Atlanta, Charlotte, Nashville, Tampa) without any new mill capex. This is the highest leverage move in the four phase plan.

Section 6

Four phases. Zero new capital investment required. All within the current 5 mill, 5 warehouse network.

Next 90 Days · FY27 Q1
Pull Sysco-CTX and Sysco-NTX account histories. Quantify competitive vs program vs distributor consolidation. Stabilize the top 5 customer losses by end of FY27 Q1. The DFW market is healthy at +5.3% YoY, so this is recoverable.

Phase 2 · Win the Southeast Corridor
3 to 12 Months · FY27 Q1 - Q4
Evaluate the proposed Atlanta warehouse cost-to-serve case (biggest SE leverage available, no mill capex). Deepen GFS Shepherdsville where Dean Psyhogios is already running +122.5% YoY. Owner: Gus Patrick + Melissa Magsalin · James Mueting

Section 7

How I pressure tested it

The ranking holds under four sensitivity lenses. Lens A (default strategic weighting) gives Houston #1 and DFW #2. Lens B (pure TAM opportunity) gives Houston #1 again, with Atlanta moving into #2 because of the cold spot. Lens C (urgency, biggest current bleed) puts DFW #1 because of the Sysco execution losses, with Tampa #2 because of the long haul rail penalty. Lens D (trajectory, where momentum is) highlights Atlanta and Tampa because of the GFS +122.5% and the GMI +13.9% growth signals.

The actionable insight: Houston wins under every weighting because the TAM gap is too large to ignore. DFW is the most urgent fix. The four phase plan respects both.

I also pressure tested with the AEs directly. Gus Patrick on the Texas execution diagnosis. Dean Psyhogios on the GFS Shepherdsville trajectory. Josh Cohen on the Nashville playbook. Each one shaped the framing of the recommendation in real ways.

Section 8

What I learned

The data is sufficient, the conversations are necessary. Region Full data from Tableau told me Sysco Texas was down 38%. Gus Patrick told me why. Both pieces were necessary.

Bounded scope is a feature, not a bug. Knowing on day one that a new mill was not on the table forced the recommendation toward the moves that are actually available: distributor partnership deepening, warehouse network optimization, toll-milling feasibility, and AE playbook replication. The constraint made the analysis sharper.

The trajectory matters more than the position. Atlanta GFS at +122.5% YoY is worth more strategic attention than a static "we have low share here" datapoint. Where the line is going beats where the dot is sitting.
