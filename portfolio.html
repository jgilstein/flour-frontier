How the project came together

The Brief

A ten-year question

Looking at the top 50 metros currently, forecast ten years out, where is flour growth going to come from, using the network we already have. Where do we put our people, our distributor relationships, and our existing capacity to get the most back?

The approach

How I worked through it

1
Sized the map. The fifty largest metros, pulled from Circana, Tableau, and census growth data, laid over our five mills and six warehouses.
2
Read our own book. Account-level volume and value by territory, distributor, and channel, in CWT and RNS.
3
Scored the markets. Six plain factors, weighted for a ten-year view, producing a ranked list and four stress tests to make sure the order held.
4
Called the field. Sat down with every single AE. Their context changed the read more than any single spreadsheet did.
What the data said

Four things the numbers made clear

Houston is the biggest opening we have
A 60 million pound market where we hold less than half our national share, and the most ethnically mixed major metro in Texas with large-scale Hispanic, Asian, and African operator demand. The gap is not appetite, it is mill proximity and pricing. Closing even part of it is worth roughly 9 million pounds a year from one city.
Texas is still worth winning, just differently
We lost the Sysco distributor-label business to Ardent, but Dallas keeps growing and our branded flour is still holding across Texas distributors. The answer is to rebuild Texas around the market that is already there, not chase a contract that is not realistically coming back soon.
The Southeast is where momentum lives
Atlanta, Tampa, Charlotte, and Nashville sit in the strongest region in the data, but the current network does not serve the corridor evenly. Orlando already shows what closer support can do in Florida. Atlanta would change the math for the rest of the Southeast.
Freight matters, but product fit matters too
Freight came up in nearly every conversation, especially in the West and Southeast. But the field also made clear that some opportunities depend on having the right product set in the first place, not just a lower delivered cost.
What the Field Added

What the conversations changed

The field work turned the project from a map into a recommendation. The conversations sharpened where freight is the real issue, where product fit matters more than price, and where GMI is leaving good business on the table because of portfolio gaps or operating rules.

Those conversations also made the final story more practical. They pushed Texas toward a market rebuild, strengthened the Atlanta warehouse case, made Houston more specific, and gave the West a clearer path around relationship-led growth and a more intentional specialty flour position. For a complete breakdown by AE and FSI management, visit the field insights page.

Product fit

Some opportunities need a better product match

One theme came up more than once. In some bakery segments, the issue is not only freight or price. Product fit matters. Across multiple territories, ethnic bread and bakery applications raised questions about dough stability and whether an enzyme-based system performs better than a malt-based one. In other accounts, especially donuts, buns, and related bakery items, shelf life and day-two quality matter just as much as flour cost. That does not change the ranking, but it does change how realistic some opportunities are. Cleaner-label, enzyme-based, and untreated options could help GMI win back some of what has been lost on larger accounts.
What I built

The deliverables

A deck for the panel, a one-page executive summary, an analysis workbook, an interactive map of all fifty metros, and this website. Each one tells the same story at a different level of detail, from a thirty-second read to the full build.

What I learned

The part the data could not show

The best ideas did not all come from a dashboard. A field day in Southern California with Nancy Muller showed how independent Hispanic bakeries buy, how a distributor route actually runs, and why a relationship can matter more than a price sheet. The macro data tells you which markets are big. The field tells you how to win them.

The handoff

What the team can pick up next

The most useful next step is a real cost-to-serve case for the Atlanta warehouse, since nearly every Southeast conversation came back to it. After that: rebuild Texas around the market, a Houston growth plan with Gus Patrick, and a West Coast playbook built on the relationship-led approach described by the field. A recurring operational fix raised across territories is moving the direct minimum from three trucks to two, which would reopen a set of profitable, low-maintenance accounts.
