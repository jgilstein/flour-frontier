How the project came together

The Brief

A ten-year question

Looking at the top 50 metros currently, forecast ten years out, where is flour growth going to come from, using the network we already have. Where do we put our people, our distributor relationships, and our existing capacity to get the most back?

The Approach

How I worked through it

1
Sized the map. The fifty largest metros, pulled from Circana, Tableau, and census growth data, laid over our five mills and six warehouses.
2
Read our own book. Account-level volume and value by territory, distributor, and channel, in CWT and RNS.
3
Scored the markets. Six plain factors, weighted for a ten-year view, producing a ranked list and four stress tests to make sure the order held.
4
Called the field. Sat down with every single AE. Their context changed the read more than any single spreadsheet did.
What the Data Said

Four things the numbers made clear

Houston is the biggest opening we have
A 60 million pound market where we hold less than half our national share. No other city has that much room.
Texas is still worth winning, just differently
We lost the Sysco distributor-label business to Ardent, but Dallas keeps growing and our branded flour is holding. The focus should be to rebuild Texas around the market that is already in place.
The Southeast is where momentum lives
Atlanta, Tampa, Charlotte, and Nashville sit in the strongest region in the data, but the current network does not serve the corridor evenly. Orlando helps Florida. Atlanta would change the math for the rest of the Southeast.
Freight decides more than flour price does
Almost everywhere we lose, we lose on the cost to get product there, not on the product itself. That single thread ran through nearly every conversation.
What the Field Added

What the AEs told me

Yoel Sharabi · West Coast
Freight, not flour price, decides the West. In the Bay Area, local mills sit an hour away and ship for almost nothing, so a truck from Vernon or Great Falls rarely pencils out. The real opening is Oregon, Washington, and the mid-sized cities between Sacramento and Portland, where operators want samples, troubleshooting, and a steady couple of trucks a month more than they want the lowest book price.
Melissa Magsalin · North Texas & Oklahoma
Independent distributors carry this market. Lasanti Foodservice out of Dallas keeps growing and pulls Gold Medal and All Trumps deep into pizzerias and Italian kitchens. Where we lose, we lose on price against nearby millers, not on quality. The Sysco North Texas loss alone moved roughly 25,000 EQC starting last fall.
Jeffrey Santiago · Florida
Distributors stayed loyal even without a private label, and operators choose us on quality when the freight is close enough. The product needs a recipe tuned for Cuban, Puerto Rican, and Mexican baking, and a reformulation would create even more growth. Orlando’s warehouse is working so well it has run up against its own capacity.
Josh Cohen · Southeast
Pizza, bakery, and manufacturing demand is all here, but Ardent in Chattanooga and bulk out of Kansas City set the price to beat. White Lily still owns Southern baking and we have no exact match for it. An Atlanta warehouse would change the math across the whole region.
Melissa Tiemeyer · East Zone Manager
The Atlanta and Orlando warehouses already proved the point: put product closer and the freight problem shrinks. Atlanta is the natural halfway point as Northeast demand drifts south, and Pittsburgh’s pizzeria scene is stronger than its size suggests. The East team is talented but stretched thin across dense, competitive ground.
Kevin Karbo · Western PA, Western NY & WV
Stay close to pizzerias, bakeries, and college-driven demand around markets like Pitt, Penn State, WVU, and Buffalo University. He also pointed to a product gap worth watching: enzyme-based shelf life solutions could help us compete more effectively in donuts, buns, and other retail bakery items against players like Don’s and BakeMark.
Nancy Muller · Southern California
Nancy’s view was that Southern California is not just a freight problem. It is also a portfolio-fit problem. In the West, cleaner-label, untreated, unmalted, and enzyme-treated flours matter more, especially in ethnic markets. Her better targets were practical ones: universities like UCLA, larger ethnic bakery pockets, and direct accounts that could work at a two-truck rhythm instead of today’s three-truck minimum.
Catherine Colon · Mentor, Sales Operations
Flour reprices every two weeks. Mixes get priced once, with freight to every destination baked in at the same time. That one difference explains much of why mix economics feel harder than flour, and it shaped how I compared the two.
Product Fit

Some opportunities need a better product match

One theme came up more than once. In some bakery segments, the issue is not only freight or price. Product fit matters. Across multiple territories, ethnic bread and bakery applications raised questions about dough stability and whether an enzyme-based system performs better than a malt-based one. In other accounts, especially donuts, buns, and related bakery items, shelf life and day-two quality matter just as much as flour cost. That does not change the ranking, but it does change how realistic some opportunities are. Cleaner-label, enzyme-based, and untreated options could help GMI win back some of what has been lost on larger accounts.
What I Built

The deliverables

A deck for the panel, a one-page executive summary, an analysis workbook, an interactive map of all fifty metros, and this website. Each one tells the same story at a different level of detail, from a thirty-second read to the full build.

What I Learned

The part the data could not show

The best ideas did not all come from a dashboard. A field day in Southern California with Nancy Muller showed how independent Hispanic bakeries buy, how a distributor route actually runs, and why a relationship can matter more than a price sheet. The macro data tells you which markets are big. The field tells you how to win them.

The Handoff

What the team can pick up next

The most useful next step is a real cost-to-serve case for the Atlanta warehouse, since nearly every Southeast conversation came back to it. After that: rebuild Texas around the market, a Houston growth plan with Gus Patrick, and a West Coast playbook built on the relationship-led approach Yoel and Nancy described. Every piece is meant to run inside the network we already have.
